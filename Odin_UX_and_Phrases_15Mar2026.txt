PRIVACY MECHANIC — KEY PRODUCT DECISION (15 Mar 2026)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

CONTEXT:
This is one of the most important product decisions in Odin's design.
It defines how knowledge travels through the network and how privacy is handled.

THE MECHANIC — CONFIRMED:
  - Stanley is friends with Aden.
  - Aden is friends with Person X (not connected to Stanley).
  - Person X adds an item, sets visibility to "Friends."
  - Aden sees it and saves it.
  - Stanley sees the item in his feed because Aden saved it.
  - Stanley does NOT see who added it — only that Aden saved it.

This is "save inheritance" — Aden's save carries the item one hop further,
anonymously. The trust chain stays clean: Stanley trusts Aden,
Aden curated it, that's enough signal. The original adder's identity is protected.

WHY THIS IS THE RIGHT DESIGN:
  - Protects original adder's identity beyond their intended circle.
  - Keeps trust chain to exactly one degree — never more.
  - Makes the curating friend (Aden) the signal, not the source.
  - Content travels further than intended — but anonymously and transparently.
  - This is Odin's core differentiator: trusted signal without privacy violation.

CONSENT & TRANSPARENCY — CONFIRMED DECISION:
  Be transparent about this mechanic upfront.
  When a user sets visibility to "Friends" on the Add page, show this tooltip/helper text:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ "Your friends can save this, and their circle will see it was   │
  │  saved — but not who added it."                                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  This is the consent moment. One sentence. Clear, honest, no fine print.
  This turns a potential privacy liability into a FEATURE — users know
  exactly how their knowledge travels, which reinforces Odin's trust promise.

TESTING FOCUS:
  When testing with users, pay close attention to:
  - Do users understand what "Friends" visibility means after reading the tooltip?
  - Do users feel comfortable knowing their save can travel one hop anonymously?
  - Do users feel the tooltip is reassuring or alarming?
  - Does the anonymous attribution feel fair or frustrating when receiving content?
  - Key moment: when a user sees an item with no attributed source — do they
    trust it less, or does "saved by [friend]" provide enough signal?
